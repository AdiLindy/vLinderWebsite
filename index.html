SPOTLIGHT

For the last couple of months, I’ve been working on an electric skateboard I built from scratch. This means I conceptualized it, selected components, CADed the incasing, designed the PCB, installed the hardware, soldered and wired the electronics, assembled the larger parts, and coded the processor. The idea is it can be used to get around smaller distances to save gas, and if the college allows it, navigate campuses. Most electric skateboards have a very short battery life because they have to fit in a tight spot under the skateboard. I wanted mine to last longer and operate at a high voltage, so instead I bought a larger battery and moved it from the bottom to the top of a longboard in its encasing. This not only allowed me a larger range but cleared up space in the incasing which I would need.
    The board includes:
  • 35mph motors
  • magnetic brakes
  • 36v 10ah battery
  • 12v headlights + speakers
  • drawer with servo lock
  • space-grade aluminum back plate
  • acceleration control
  • 20m transmitting range
  • physical spedometer
  • speed saftey trigger

CAD

  • SOLIDWORKS
  • 3Dexpeirnce
  • onShape
  • Fusion 360
  • Z-Brush
  • onShape
  • Sketch Up

However, I often work on 3D experience or onShape. I enjoy CAD because it's a very direct and quick way of turing my thoughts into conceivable models. CADing is often my favorite part of the building process, and on top of that, I love fabrication, especially automated fabrication because of the intricacy and math those machines require.
